Word-watching commentary

This distant reading project looks at the last 5 issues (volume 49 through 53) from Computers and Composition. Word clouds demonstrating word frequency from the 100 most used words were created using TagCrowd. Volumes 49, 50, and 52 each generated multiple word clouds because the publication size exceeded the plaintext word limit of TagCrowd. Word cloud images have been included both as screen shot thumbnails and as HTML embeds. Almost every word cloud grouping, even those split among several volumes, focus on students, writing, composition, digital, and computers. Given the name of the journal is Computers and Compositon, the emphasis on both composition/writing and computers/digital do not seem surprising. I was mildly surprised at the frequency of students, although other common themes of university, classroom, and instructor help put the journal's focus a bit more in context.

What did you see in these word clouds? (we may not use these as headings)

While certain elements remain relatively consistent across journals, each journal has a noticeable theme. For example, Volume 53 is titled "Digital Technologies, Bodies, and Embodiments" and some emerging trends in this issue that do not appear in others can be seen with words like "bodies," "violence," "sexual," "harassment," "metoo" "feminist," and "social." While gender and feminism seem to have some prominence here, they are much more prominent in Volume 51, "Technofeminism: (Re)Generations and Intersectional Futures". This volume also uses words like "bodies," "feminist," and "social," but "violence" and sexuality are nowhere to be found (at least in the 100 most frequently used words in the volume.

What questions, patterns, or insights did your word clouds provoke?

Each journal firmly stands at the intersection of writing and technology with a heavy focus on students, classroom practice, and writing. Recent issues seem to focus more on feminism, embodiment, bodies in ways that seem quite on the opposite side of abstract forms of digital, technology, and rhetoric.

How has this distant reading helped you make sense of "digital rhetoric"?

I'm still not quite sure I've got a firm grasp on digital rhetoric, but it's clear that Computers and Composition sees very clear, persistent connections between writing and technology. While that might seem obvious at the paratextual level (it is called Computers and Composition), this word cloud really cements that focus while teasing out some unexpected elements that might be worth pursuing in future research.
